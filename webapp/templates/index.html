The Search

Humans have long sought to answer the age-old question, "Are we alone?". Researchers estimate that hundreds of billions of exoplanets exist within our galaxy, including tens of billions which are similar to our Earth. Despite our quiet existence, the likelihood that we are not alone is greater than many would think.

 

Today, astronomers around the globe are collaborating in the search for extraterrestrial intelligence (SETI). Armed with some of the most powerful radio telescopes, computing resources, and the smartest algorithms we have, BL@Scale (Built by the UC Berkeley SETI Research Center) is taking our endeavors to the cloud.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The Status Quo

SETI programs search for technosignatures - indicators of technology as a proxy for intelligence.

 

Technosignatures

One class of technosignature is signals generated by radio transmitters. Existing searches have largely focused on narrow-band signals (with widths less than 100 Hz or so), since these do not occur naturally in astronomy. However, many human-produced sources of radio emission are also narrow-band. We can reject a large fraction of this interference by searching for signals that are drifting in frequency, which indicates that they are less likely to be stationary with respect to the receiver, and hence more likely to be originating from a distant transmitter that is in motion. Classical algorithms, such as TurboSETI, exploit this characteristic to search for these types of signals by correcting radio spectrograms for a range of possible Doppler shifts, and picking signals that appear above a certain signal to noise ratio.

 

A second key characteristic of technosignatures is that they are isolated to certain positions on the sky. A signal that is present in the data regardless of where the telescope is pointed is unlikely to be from ETI. We compare observations of our target stars with observations of nearby positions to search for signals that track the motion of the target star across the sky.

Thirdly, technosignatures might appear qualitatively somewhat similar to other signals in the data, or alternatively they might appear highly anomalous. The development of generalized anomaly detection algorithms is a key goal of modern SETI.

 

On-Premise Data Centers

Most large scale searches to date have been performed on high performance computing clusters on campus or at the observatories. Traditionally we've scaled our computing resources by physically expanding the datacenter's capabilities, but as the data begin to fill up petabytes of disk space, there is a pressing need to also scale up the search capacity.

The Problem

1) We need a variety of algorithms to search for a wider range of possible SETI signals, and 2) we need to develop infrastructure to scale these searches to deal with petascale data volumes.

 

We are developing a flexible platform to implement a variety of techniques and computational processes to filter and clean the data, and to search for signals. A key motivation is to allow easy deployment of promising new algorithms, including those that may not even exist at present.

 

Scaling

We need a system that can scale resources dynamically with an increase in demand, such as when an analysis of our entire multi-petabyte dataset is underway.

The Solution

We want to develop an armada of smarter search tools borrowing signal processing and machine learning techniques from industry. We also want to develop a cloud-based infrastructure to seamlessly handle computing demand and to deploy algorithms across large datastores.

 

Modern Search Techniques

In order to accomplish the ambitious goal of developing smarter search techniques, we're implementing machine learning algorithms to expand our search domain beyond narrowband doppler drifting search techniques. Machine learning approaches also promise increased potential to filter out human-generated interference signals using new techniques.

 

BL@Scale

This current platform is sitting on top of a bedrock of infrastructure built to facilitate, scale, and execute these modern search techniques by pushing it to the cloud. Using technologies like Google's GCP, Kubernetes, and other high performing computing resources, we are able to bring SETI searches to the next generation.

To access this feature contact the UCBerkeleySETI group for more information.

Research and Support

BL@Scale is built by a team at the UC Berkeley SETI Research Center. For more information, visit our website as well as our in-depth tutorials at here.

 

The Berkeley SETI Research Center conducts experiments searching for electromagnetic signatures of intelligent extraterrestrial civilizations, spanning wavelengths from radio to visible light. We're the creators of the SETI@home volunteer computing software, that utilizes spare resources on volunteer computers to aid in processing data from radio telescopes.

 

We're participating in the new Breakthrough Listen initiative to use the Green Bank, Parkes, and Automated Planet Finder telescopes to dramatically expand the search for cosmic company.

 

 

BL Reservoir

BL Reservoir is a repository of powerful tools we've developed in house to search through our data in new and different ways. We're currently experimenting with deep learning and unsupervised techniques to detect anomalies. This is accomplished with a high dimensional clustering of encoded features extracted from window snippets of the radio spectrogram.

 

Another search method is the Energy Detection algorithm used to filter for signals of high statistical anomaly based on signal power.

 

The goal is to develop a framework that can plug in any algorithm a researcher might write and execute it at scale!
